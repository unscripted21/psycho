🧠 personality · defence · growth

100 integrated questions (5–10 marks) – answers in point form – nursing focus
1Define personality. Explain its origin and core elements. 5 marks
  • Derived from Latin ‘persona’ – theatrical mask.
  • Includes outer self (behaviour) and inner self (thoughts, feelings).
  • Sum total of physical abilities and mental traits.
  • Unique, persistent over time.
  • Characteristic pattern that distinguishes one person from another.
2List four characteristics of personality. 5 marks
  • Unique – no two individuals identical.
  • Relatively stable qualities over time.
  • Dynamic orientation to environment.
  • Greatly influenced by social interaction.
3Discuss the three main determinants of personality. 8 marks
  • Heredity – factors determined at conception (physical structure, gender, temperament, energy level).
  • Environment – culture, early conditioning, family norms, social groups.
  • Situation/time – personality changes with varying demands of situations; not in isolation.
4Explain the psychodynamic theory of personality according to Freud. 8 marks
  • Personality influenced by unconscious psychological forces.
  • Three structures: id (pleasure principle), ego (reality), superego (moral standards).
  • Conflict between id and superego creates intrapsychic conflict.
  • Healthy personality requires balance.
  • Libido (sexual energy) drives psychosexual stages; fixation may occur if unresolved.
5Describe the roles of id, ego and superego. 6 marks
  • Id – primitive, instinctual; seeks immediate pleasure; unconscious.
  • Ego – realistic mediator; balances id and superego; uses defence mechanisms.
  • Superego – moral conscience; internalises societal rules; strives for perfection.
6What are defence mechanisms? State their key features. 5 marks
  • Unconscious psychological strategies.
  • Reduce anxiety, internal conflict, emotional distress.
  • Protect the ego.
  • Distort reality to varying degrees.
  • First described by Sigmund Freud, expanded by Anna Freud.
7Why are defence mechanisms important in nursing? Give four reasons. 6 marks
  • Help avoid misinterpreting patient behaviour.
  • Improve therapeutic communication.
  • Reduce conflict.
  • Promote coping and support mental health.
8List the four levels of defence mechanisms with an example each. 8 marks
  • Primitive – psychosis‑level denial (highly distorted reality).
  • Immature – acting out (common in adolescents).
  • Neurotic – repression (common in adults).
  • Mature – sublimation (healthy coping).
9Differentiate between maladaptive and adaptive defence mechanisms. Give two examples each. 7 marks
  • Maladaptive: persistent denial, projection, acting out, splitting – hinder coping, distort reality.
  • Adaptive: sublimation, humour, suppression, altruism – reduce anxiety constructively, flexible.
10Define denial and give a clinical example. State nursing interventions. 6 marks
  • Refusing to accept reality/facts.
  • Example: cancer patient says “the lab made a mistake”.
  • Interventions: do not confront aggressively; provide factual info gradually; allow time for adjustment; support emotional processing.
11Explain repression. Give a clinical example and nursing application. 5 marks
  • Unconsciously blocking painful thoughts/memories.
  • Example: patient cannot recall traumatic ICU experience.
  • Nursing: avoid forcing recall; offer emotional support; recognise PTSD risk.
12Describe projection with an example and how nurse should respond. 6 marks
  • Attributing one’s own feelings to others.
  • Example: patient afraid of surgery says “you nurses seem nervous”.
  • Nursing: respond calmly; do not become defensive; clarify feelings gently.
13What is displacement? Give healthcare example and nursing action. 5 marks
  • Redirecting emotions toward safer target.
  • Example: angry about diagnosis shouts at nurse.
  • Nursing: don’t personalise anger; acknowledge feelings; maintain professional boundaries.
14Define regression. Provide example and nursing intervention. 5 marks
  • Reverting to earlier developmental behaviour.
  • Example: adult becomes childlike after surgery.
  • Nursing: encourage independence gradually; provide reassurance; avoid fostering dependency.
15Explain rationalisation. Give a clinical example and nursing approach. 6 marks
  • Justifying behaviour with logical excuses.
  • Example: smoker with COPD says “everyone dies anyway”.
  • Nursing: explore ambivalence; use motivational interviewing; avoid arguing.
16Describe reaction formation. Example and nursing role. 5 marks
  • Acting opposite to true feelings.
  • Example: overly cheerful after terminal diagnosis.
  • Nursing: watch for hidden distress; provide safe space for expression.
17What is suppression? How is it different from repression? Give example. 6 marks
  • Suppression – conscious decision to delay dealing with emotion (e.g., “I’ll think after surgery”).
  • Repression – unconscious blocking.
  • Nursing: respect coping style; follow up later.
18Define sublimation and give an example. Why is it mature? 5 marks
  • Channeling negative impulses into positive activity.
  • Example: anger → joins support group.
  • Mature because it transforms unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable behaviour.
19Explain intellectualization with a clinical example. Nursing intervention? 6 marks
  • Focusing on facts to avoid emotional distress.
  • Example: patient talks only about lab values after bad diagnosis.
  • Nursing: acknowledge knowledge; gently explore emotions.
20What are identification and compensation? Give examples. 6 marks
  • Identification: adopting traits of admired person (child with chronic illness wants to be a nurse).
  • Compensation: overachieving to offset weakness (amputee excels academically).
  • Nursing: encourage positive role modelling; reinforce strengths, promote realistic self-image.
21List common defence mechanisms in psychiatric nursing. How to manage splitting? 7 marks
  • Common: projection, splitting, denial, acting out.
  • Splitting: staff seen as ‘good’ or ‘bad’.
  • Management: maintain team communication; set consistent boundaries.
22Identify defences often seen in oncology nursing and nursing role. 5 marks
  • Denial, intellectualization, rationalization.
  • Role: provide info gradually; offer emotional support; monitor depression risk.
23What defences appear in critical care nursing? Appropriate interventions? 6 marks
  • Dissociation, repression, denial.
  • Interventions: assess psychological trauma; encourage debriefing; involve family support.
24Describe common paediatric defences and nursing approaches. 6 marks
  • Regression, fantasy, acting out.
  • Approaches: play therapy; involve parents; provide reassurance.
25Explain defences in geriatric nursing and nursing role. 6 marks
  • Denial of memory loss, rationalization, withdrawal.
  • Role: screen for depression; maintain dignity; support autonomy.
26How do nurses themselves use defence mechanisms? Give examples. 5 marks
  • Emotional detachment (isolation), humour, suppression, burnout-related cynicism.
  • Self‑awareness prevents compassion fatigue, countertransference, exhaustion.
27State the goal of nursing regarding maladaptive vs adaptive coping. 5 marks
  • Support transition from maladaptive to adaptive coping.
  • Encourage healthy strategies (sublimation, humour) and reduce persistent denial/projection.
28List five ‘do’s’ and four ‘avoid’ in therapeutic communication with defence mechanisms. 8 marks
  • Do: use empathy; reflect feelings; maintain neutrality; avoid confrontation; provide safe environment.
  • Avoid: arguing with denial; taking projection personally; reinforcing regression.
29Analyse the case: 45-year-old post‑MI says “it’s indigestion, back to work tomorrow”. Identify defence and nursing actions. 6 marks
  • Defence: denial.
  • Nursing: provide education; discuss risk factors; encourage gradual adjustment; monitor compliance.
30Explain the Big Five personality traits (OCEAN). 7 marks
  • Openness – creativity, curiosity.
  • Conscientiousness – organisation, responsibility.
  • Extraversion – sociability, energy.
  • Agreeableness – compassion, cooperation.
  • Neuroticism – anxiety, emotional instability.
31How is openness to experience relevant in nursing? Give example. 5 marks
  • Accepts new guidelines, adapts to technology, culturally sensitive.
  • Example: nurse quickly learns new electronic health records and trains others.
32Why is conscientiousness crucial in nursing practice? Provide example. 5 marks
  • Ensures accurate medication, proper documentation, infection control, reduces errors.
  • Example: double‑checking dosage prevents drug error.
33Describe how extraversion benefits community health nursing. 5 marks
  • Effective patient education, leading outreach, encouraging patients.
  • Example: community nurse confidently addresses maternal health group.
34Explain the importance of agreeableness in nurse–patient relationships. 5 marks
  • Builds therapeutic rapport, manages difficult patients calmly, improves teamwork.
  • Example: calmly listening to grieving mother.
35How does neuroticism affect a nurse’s performance? Give example. 5 marks
  • High neuroticism → anxiety, burnout, poor decisions under pressure.
  • Emotionally stable nurse remains calm during cardiac arrest.
36Summarise Gordon Allport’s trait categories with nursing examples. 7 marks
  • Cardinal: dominant trait defining life (e.g., Mother Teresa’s compassion).
  • Central: 5–10 general traits (honesty, kindness – reliable health records officer).
  • Secondary: situational (calm nurse anxious only during exams).
37What are Cattell’s 16 source traits? Give two examples relevant to healthcare. 6 marks
  • Traits like warm/reserved, dominant/submissive, emotionally stable/reactive.
  • Example: dominant hospital administrator leads department effectively.
38Explain Eysenck’s three personality dimensions. Give nursing examples. 7 marks
  • Extraversion/introversion: outgoing clinical officer vs introverted lab tech.
  • Neuroticism/emotional stability: easily stressed vs calm in emergencies.
  • Psychoticism: tough‑mindedness, aggressiveness.
39How can trait theory be applied in staff recruitment and team building? 6 marks
  • Conscientious for medication wards; agreeable for counselling; emotionally stable for ICU.
  • Mix extraverts/introverts for balance.
40List strengths and limitations of trait theory in nursing. 6 marks
  • Strengths: measurable, easy to understand, predicts behaviour, useful in career guidance.
  • Limitations: ignores situational influences, oversimplifies, doesn’t explain development.
41Define human growth and development. Differentiate growth vs development. 5 marks
  • Growth: measurable biological increase (height, weight, organ size).
  • Development: functional/qualitative changes (skills, emotions, behaviour).
  • Both lifelong, multidimensional, sequential.
42Explain four concepts/principles of human development. 5 marks
  • Lifelong process – conception to death.
  • Multidimensional – physical, cognitive, psychosocial.
  • Sequential and predictable – cephalocaudal, proximodistal.
  • Multiple factors – genetics, environment, culture.
43Why is understanding growth and development relevant to nursing? 6 marks
  • Provide age‑appropriate care.
  • Anticipate patient needs.
  • Support health promotion.
  • Communicate effectively across life stages.
44List six factors affecting growth and development with examples. 7 marks
  • Genetic: tall parents → tall child.
  • Prenatal: rubella → congenital defects.
  • Nutrition: malnutrition → kwashiorkor.
  • Health/disease: recurrent malaria → delayed growth.
  • Socio‑economic: poverty limits healthcare access.
  • Family support: neglect → low self‑esteem.
45Describe the stages of human development with Kenyan examples. 8 marks
  • Infancy (0–2): immunisation at KNH.
  • Early childhood (2–6): ECDE play‑based learning.
  • Middle childhood (6–12): CBC in primary schools.
  • Adolescence (12–18): Shujaaz youth programmes.
  • Early adulthood (18–40): nursing students.
  • Middle adulthood (40–65): rising hypertension in urban Kenya.
  • Late adulthood (65+): community support in Nyeri.
46Summarise Erikson’s psychosocial theory. Give a Kenyan example for one stage. 6 marks
  • Eight stages of psychosocial conflict (trust vs mistrust, identity vs role confusion, etc.).
  • Example: adolescents in Kenya facing identity crisis due to cultural vs modern pressures.
47Explain Piaget’s cognitive development stages. Relate to CBC in Kenya. 6 marks
  • Sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational.
  • Kenyan CBC uses hands-on learning (concrete operational) in primary schools.
48What is Bronfenbrenner’s ecological systems theory? Give a nursing student example. 5 marks
  • Development influenced by multiple environments: family, school, community, policy.
  • Example: nursing student in Nairobi shaped by family, university, and national health policies.
49Discuss nursing implications of growth and development knowledge. 6 marks
  • Holistic care – consider physical, emotional, social aspects.
  • Age‑specific care – paediatric vs geriatric.
  • Community health – education, vaccination, maternal‑child programmes.
  • Ethical considerations – respect culture while promoting evidence‑based care.
50How can a nurse use knowledge of personality traits to improve patient care? 6 marks
  • Adapt communication style (introvert needs privacy).
  • Motivate treatment compliance (conscientious patients need organisation).
  • Manage behaviour (high neuroticism needs reassurance).
  • Plan individualised care.
51Differentiate between Freud’s id, ego and superego. How do they relate to defence mechanisms? 7 marks
  • Id – pleasure principle; superego – moral standards; ego – mediator.
  • When conflict creates anxiety, ego deploys defence mechanisms (unconscious).
  • Defences protect ego and reduce anxiety.
52What does “intrapsychic conflict” mean? Give example. 5 marks
  • Conflict within the mind, usually between id and superego.
  • Example: wanting to eat unhealthy food (id) vs guilt (superego).
53Explain how an overly strong id or superego affects personality. 6 marks
  • Strong id → rude, selfish, animalistic, possibly psychopathic.
  • Strong superego → chronic worry, guilt, neurotic, represses desires.
54Describe Jung’s contribution to psychodynamic theory. Mention archetypes. 6 marks
  • Expanded libido to psychic energy of all life forces.
  • Introduced collective unconscious and archetypes (e.g., persona).
  • Attitudes: extroverted/introverted; rational/irrational.
55What did Adler emphasise about personality development? Define inferiority complex. 5 marks
  • Personality shaped by compensation – efforts to overcome weakness/inferiority.
  • Inferiority complex: inability to overcome feelings of inferiority.
56Explain Karen Horney’s view on personality and three neurotic trends. 6 marks
  • Anxiety from sexual and non‑sexual childhood conflicts.
  • Three neurotic trends: submission (moving toward), aggression (against), detachment (away from).
57Outline Erikson’s Eight Ages of Man. Which stage involves identity crisis? 7 marks
  • Trust vs mistrust, autonomy vs shame, initiative vs guilt, industry vs inferiority, identity vs role confusion (adolescence), intimacy vs isolation, generativity vs stagnation, integrity vs despair.
58How can the Big Five be used in nursing education? 5 marks
  • Help students understand strengths/weaknesses.
  • Encourage self‑reflection.
  • Guide career specialisation (e.g., organised → records, empathetic → palliative).
59Interpret a Big Five profile: Openness 16, Conscientiousness 18, Extraversion 12, Agreeableness 17, Neuroticism 8. 6 marks
  • Openness high – curious, adapts to technology.
  • Conscientiousness very high – organised, reliable, reduces errors.
  • Extraversion moderate – balanced, works alone or with teams.
  • Agreeableness high – empathetic, strong communication.
  • Neuroticism low – emotionally stable, handles emergencies.
60What are the strengths and limitations of the Big Five model in clinical settings? 5 marks
  • Strengths: widely accepted, predicts behaviour, helps team composition.
  • Limitations: traits may change with experience; cultural differences; doesn’t consider situation.
61List five prenatal factors affecting development. 5 marks
  • Maternal nutrition, infections (rubella), drug/alcohol use, stress, chronic illness.
62How does nutrition influence growth and development? Give examples. 5 marks
  • Balanced diet supports healthy growth; malnutrition causes stunting, kwashiorkor, marasmus.
  • Iron deficiency → anaemia, poor cognition.
63Explain the impact of socio‑economic status on child development. 5 marks
  • High SES → better nutrition, healthcare, education → optimal development.
  • Low SES → limited resources, increased stress, developmental delays.
64Describe how culture and religion affect growth and development. 5 marks
  • Influence diet, child‑rearing, education, marriage age (e.g., early marriage interrupts education).
  • Beliefs may affect healthcare seeking.
65What is the role of family and social support in development? 5 marks
  • Emotional bonding, love, security promote healthy psychological development.
  • Neglect leads to low self‑esteem, attachment issues.
66How does physical activity influence growth? Provide an example. 5 marks
  • Supports muscle, bone growth, motor coordination.
  • Example: children playing outdoors develop better fitness than sedentary peers.
67Give examples of life events/trauma that disrupt development. 5 marks
  • Parental loss, abuse, war, natural disasters → anxiety, behavioural problems.
68How do gender differences affect growth and development? 5 marks
  • Girls typically enter puberty earlier than boys.
  • Hormonal differences influence physical and emotional development.
69What is the cephalocaudal principle? Give example. 5 marks
  • Development proceeds from head to tail (head to toe).
  • Example: infants gain head control before sitting.
70Explain the proximodistal principle with example. 5 marks
  • Development proceeds from centre of body outward.
  • Example: infants control shoulders before fingers.
71How can a nurse use trait theory to handle an introverted patient? 5 marks
  • Provide private, quiet environment.
  • Allow time to process information.
  • Use one‑to‑one communication rather than groups.
72What nursing areas suit a person high in conscientiousness? 5 marks
  • Medication administration, health records, ICU, surgical wards – roles requiring precision, documentation.
73Why might a nurse high in neuroticism need additional support? 5 marks
  • Prone to anxiety, stress, burnout; may struggle in emergencies.
  • Need mentoring, stress management, rotation to less acute areas.
74Explain how a nurse can use the Big Five to build effective teams. 6 marks
  • Balance extraverts (outreach) and introverts (lab).
  • Place agreeable staff in patient‑facing roles.
  • Conscientious for quality improvement.
  • Emotionally stable for emergency units.
75Describe Allport’s cardinal, central, and secondary traits with nursing examples. 7 marks
  • Cardinal: defines person (e.g., Florence Nightingale’s compassion).
  • Central: 5–10 general (reliable, kind – a nurse known for honesty).
  • Secondary: situational (calm nurse anxious about public speaking).
76How can understanding patient personality improve treatment adherence? 5 marks
  • Conscientious patient: use checklists, written plans.
  • Agreeable patient: build rapport, involve family.
  • Low conscientiousness: simplify regimen, follow up closely.
77What is the difference between trait theory and psychodynamic theory? 6 marks
  • Trait: focuses on measurable, stable traits; observable.
  • Psychodynamic: emphasises unconscious conflicts, childhood, defence mechanisms.
78How can a nurse manager use personality assessment in staff development? 6 marks
  • Identify strengths for role allocation.
  • Design training (e.g., communication for introverts).
  • Support emotional stability through mentoring.
79Give an example of a patient low in agreeableness. How would you communicate? 5 marks
  • May be confrontational, distrustful.
  • Use calm, respectful tone; set clear boundaries; avoid arguing.
80What is the nursing significance of the Big Five in mental health? 6 marks
  • Assess personality predispositions (high neuroticism → anxiety risk).
  • Plan individualised care (reassurance for neurotic, structure for low conscientious).
  • Identify strengths for therapy.
81A patient says “the doctors are wrong, I’ll get a second opinion that says I’m fine.” Identify defence and nursing response. 6 marks
  • Defence: denial.
  • Response: support right to seek information, but gently ask “what if the second opinion is the same?”
82Patient awaiting biopsy says “I’m not worried, statistics show 90% benign.” Defence? Nursing reply? 5 marks
  • Intellectualization.
  • Reply: “It’s good you understand the stats – and it’s normal to feel anxious while waiting.”
83Patient with new stoma refuses to look at it. Defence and nursing interventions. 6 marks
  • Denial.
  • Interventions: build trust, gradual exposure, involve stoma therapist, allow expression of disgust/fear.
84How does a nurse recognise splitting in a psychiatric ward? What to do? 5 marks
  • Patient labels some staff ‘good’, others ‘bad’.
  • Action: team meetings to ensure consistent responses, set firm boundaries.
85Explain countertransference and how self‑awareness of defence mechanisms helps. 5 marks
  • Countertransference: nurse’s unconscious reaction to patient.
  • Self‑awareness helps nurse recognise own defences (e.g., detachment) and respond therapeutically.
86Why might a newly diagnosed diabetic patient say “I feel fine, I don’t need insulin”? Defence and nursing plan. 6 marks
  • Denial.
  • Plan: educate gently, involve diabetic educator, allow time, discuss consequences without alarm.
87What defence mechanism is at play when a nurse uses humour after a stressful code? Adaptive or maladaptive? 5 marks
  • Humour – mature/adaptive if it relieves tension and fosters connection.
  • Maladaptive if used to avoid processing grief.
88Differentiate reaction formation from undoing. 5 marks
  • Reaction formation: acting opposite to true feeling (e.g., overly cheerful when scared).
  • Undoing: ritual to ‘cancel’ an unacceptable act (e.g., compulsive handwashing after a bad thought).
89How can a nurse help a patient move from intellectualization to emotional expression? 5 marks
  • Acknowledge facts first, then gently ask: “how does that information make you feel?”
  • Create safe space, model emotional language.
90What are the three neurotic trends according to Horney? Give examples. 6 marks
  • Submission (moving toward people): seek approval.
  • Aggression (moving against): hostile, controlling.
  • Detachment (moving away): withdrawn, isolated.
91How does knowledge of defence mechanisms improve therapeutic communication? 6 marks
  • Recognise underlying anxiety, not just behaviour.
  • Avoid arguing with denial.
  • Validate feelings behind projection.
  • Provide safe environment for expression.
92Discuss the role of culture in personality expression. Use Kenyan example. 6 marks
  • Culture shapes traits (e.g., collectivism encourages agreeableness).
  • Kenyan example: respect for elders may mask disagreement (reaction formation).
  • Nurses must be culturally sensitive.
93Compare Freud’s psychosexual stages with Erikson’s psychosocial stages. 7 marks
  • Freud: oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital – focus on libido.
  • Erikson: eight stages across lifespan – focus on social relationships.
  • Both stage theories, but Erikson more socially oriented.
94How can a nurse assess whether a patient’s defence mechanism is adaptive? 5 marks
  • Is it temporary? Reduces anxiety without harming self/others?
  • Allows eventual coping? If yes, adaptive (e.g., humour).
  • If persistent and blocks reality, maladaptive (e.g., denial).
95Explain the concept of “persona” according to Jung. How is it relevant in nursing? 5 marks
  • Persona = social mask we wear in public.
  • Nurses may adopt a professional persona; important to remain authentic to avoid burnout.
96What is the value of the Big Five in understanding patient behaviour? 5 marks
  • Predict how patient may react to illness (e.g., high neuroticism → more anxiety).
  • Tailor communication and support.
97List four signs that a patient may be using repression. 5 marks
  • Gaps in memory of stressful events.
  • Becoming anxious when reminded.
  • Vague physical symptoms without cause.
  • Unexplained emotional reactions.
98Describe how a nurse manager can support a nurse showing burnout‑related cynicism (a defence). 6 marks
  • Recognise as possible defence.
  • Provide debriefing, counselling.
  • Adjust workload, encourage self‑care.
  • Foster supportive team environment.
99How does understanding growth and development help in communicating with an elderly patient? 5 marks
  • Know typical age‑related changes (slower processing).
  • Use clear, respectful language; allow time.
  • Address dignity, autonomy, and possible denial of memory loss.
100Integrate personality, defence mechanisms, and growth knowledge into a holistic nursing care plan. 10 marks
  • Assess patient’s personality traits (e.g., low conscientiousness needs reminders).
  • Identify defence mechanisms (denial → gradual education).
  • Consider developmental stage (adolescent needs identity support).
  • Tailor communication (introvert → private setting).
  • Involve family, provide age‑appropriate resources, support adaptive coping.
